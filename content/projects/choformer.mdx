## Context

Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO) cells are the workhorse of biopharmaceutical production. Codon optimization — adapting gene sequences to match host codon usage preferences — can significantly improve protein expression yields. Traditional approaches use heuristic rules; recent work suggests sequence models may capture subtler patterns.

## Question

Can a transformer architecture learn host-specific codon optimization patterns from expression data, outperforming rule-based methods?

## Method

We trained a transformer encoder on paired (original, optimized) gene sequences from published CHO expression datasets. The model learns to predict codon substitutions that maximize predicted expression while preserving amino acid sequence.

Architecture details:
- 6-layer transformer encoder
- Codon-level tokenization (64 tokens)
- Multi-task loss: expression prediction + sequence reconstruction

## Result

Early results show the model captures known CHO codon preferences (e.g., preference for G/C-ending codons) and discovers non-obvious patterns in rare codon contexts. Benchmarking against established tools (e.g., GeneOptimizer) is ongoing.

## Reflection

Codon optimization sits at an interesting intersection of biology and engineering. The "optimal" sequence depends on context — vector, promoter, cell line, protein fold — that no model fully captures. The value may be in augmenting expert judgment rather than replacing it.
