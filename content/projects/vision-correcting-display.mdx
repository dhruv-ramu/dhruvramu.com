## Context

Refractive errors (myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism) affect billions of people worldwide. Traditional correction requires lenses or surgery. Recent work in computational displays suggests that pre-distorting images on screen can compensate for optical aberrations in the eye — potentially enabling lens-free correction.

## Question

Can a computational display system pre-compensate images such that a viewer with a known refractive error perceives a sharp image without corrective lenses?

## Method

1. **Characterize the optical aberration** using a point spread function (PSF) model of the viewer's eye
2. **Compute the inverse filter** (deconvolution kernel) from the PSF
3. **Pre-distort displayed images** by convolving with the inverse kernel
4. **Validate perceptually** using test patterns and subjective sharpness ratings

## Result

Prototype demonstrations show measurable improvement in perceived sharpness for low-to-moderate myopia (-1.0 to -3.0 diopters). Performance degrades at higher corrections and is sensitive to viewing distance and head position.

## Reflection

The gap between laboratory demonstration and practical product is enormous. Head movement, binocular vision, display resolution limits, and individual variation in PSF all complicate real-world deployment. But the underlying principle — using computation to replace optics — is worth exploring.
