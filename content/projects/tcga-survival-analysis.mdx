## Context

Cancer biomarker discovery often begins with single-gene associations against survival endpoints. Yet biological systems are rarely single-gene phenomena — pathway interactions, compensatory mechanisms, and context-dependent expression all suggest that combinatorial signatures may capture prognostic signal that individual genes miss.

The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) provides transcriptomic and clinical data across dozens of cancer types, making it a natural testbed for comparing these approaches systematically.

## Question

Do combinatorial gene-expression signatures outperform single-gene markers for survival prediction across TCGA cancer types — and if so, under what conditions?

More precisely: when does adding a second (or third) gene to a signature improve prognostic separation, and when does it merely add noise?

## Method

We developed a pipeline to:

1. **Curate TCGA datasets** across selected cancer types with sufficient sample sizes and complete survival data
2. **Compute single-gene Cox proportional hazards models** for each gene against overall survival
3. **Enumerate combinatorial signatures** using greedy forward selection and correlation filtering
4. **Compare performance** using concordance index, log-rank tests, and cross-validation stability

<Callout type="note">
Sample sizes vary dramatically across TCGA cohorts. We applied minimum thresholds (n ≥ 100, events ≥ 20) before including a cancer type in the analysis.
</Callout>

## Result

Combinatorial signatures showed modest but consistent improvement over single-gene markers in ~40% of cancer types tested. The largest gains appeared in cancers with high transcriptional heterogeneity (e.g., LUAD, BRCA).

However, many combinatorial signatures failed to replicate across train/test splits — suggesting that apparent improvements often reflected overfitting rather than genuine biological signal.

| Approach | Median C-index | Reproducibility Rate |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Single-gene | 0.58 | 62% |
| 2-gene combo | 0.61 | 48% |
| 3-gene combo | 0.63 | 35% |

## Reflection

This project reinforced a uncomfortable truth: the gap between statistical significance and biological meaning is wider than most survival analyses acknowledge. Combinatorial methods can find signal, but they find noise just as readily.

The more interesting question may not be "which genes predict survival" but "under what biological conditions does combinatorial signal reflect real pathway biology versus artifact."
